Orignally, I planned this post in my head as a celebration post to celebrate the acceptance of my first paper after four years of trying. In that post, I imagined that I could happily recount all the obstacles I have overcome and how I eventually reach the happy ending; just like any typical happy ending movie one could imagine. Unfortuantely, that post will probably be forever in my mind. I had a high hope on the latest CIDR 2025 submission. But, the paper is rejected, again. After spending four years in graduate school with zero publication and five rejections of the same piece of research, I think it's time to move on.

As the title suggested, I will analyze all five rejections I have recieved and reflect the lessons I have learned along the way. To acheive the goal, I will first provide necessary background on the research itself so that I can have a basis on analyzing each rejection. There are five rejections in total that spans from 2021 to 2024. For each rejection, I first provide historical remarks on the submission itself, e.g., what's the key idea on the way of presenting the work and any story related. Then, I will walk through each review within each rejection and provide my understanding on each review, e.g., what the reviewers think of the work, what place I can further improve it, and so on.

The research is published on arXiv with keyword "TreeTracker Join" (Hu et al., 2024). Here is a table of summary on each submission and rejection.

Background on Research

Note

I will make this piece of research accessible to general audience with some background in computer science. As a result, I have to omit certain details. Some details are provided during analysis of rejections. I also prepared a pdf note, which provides more complete picture than what is described here.

This piece of research concerns join algorithm in relational database. A relational database can be thought as a collection of tables where each table has a set of columns. Rows in a table are data that the user stores. Formally, a table is a relation and columns are collectively called attributes or variables.
