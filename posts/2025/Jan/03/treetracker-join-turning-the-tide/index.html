Background on Research aka. Stories

The research is about designing a new join algorithm called TreeTracker Join (TTJ) [Hu et al., 2024] that is fast both in theory and in practice. The research starts on 10/31/2019. At that time, I was laid off from my gig at State Street and I reached out to Daniel Miranker, who I TAed for two years at UT-Austin when I was a master student, to see if he had any leads for jobs. During the initial conversations, I talked about my interests in research and some work I did with DB2 at IBM. He pitched me his vision on building a next-generation graph database, which required a mixture of theory and system development. Dancing between theory and practice is something that I have a strong interest in and I decided to do a PhD with him.

He gave me two papers: the full reducer paper [Bernstein and Chiu, 1981] and the TreeTracker paper [BayardoJr and Miranker, 1994], where Roberto Bayardo and Miranker gave an alternative optimal algorithm for solving a class of constraint satisfaction problem (CSP) that is acyclic. The key difference between TreeTracker and the piror art AC-4 is that AC-4 needs to pre-process CSP so that the resulting CSP can be solved efficiently (technical term on this is "without backtracking"). An obvious issue with any pre-processing-based algorithm is that if the input instance to the algorithm is not that bad meaning solving the problem directly can lead to the solution much quicker than the pre-processing itself, pre-processing is an overhead even though the final runtime bound is optimal. TreeTracker algorithm directly solves the CSP and perform extra steps only when it encounter failures during the solving process.

It is well-known that evaluating queries involving join is effectively the same as solving CSP [Kolaitis and Vardi, 1998, Chandra and Merlin, 1977]. The full reducer invented by Phil Bernstein and Dah-Ming W. Chiu is effectively the same as enforcing arc-consistency [Russell and Norvig, 2010 Chapter 6] in a CSP that is acyclic. Thus, any algorithm that is based on the full reducer will have some overhead just like AC-4. This implies that such algorithm has room for improvements using the TreeTracker idea; just like what it did for AC-4. Therefore, Miranker's idea is to translate TreeTracker from CSP into a join algorithm in database.

When I got assigned with the task in 2019, I had no idea about what's the corresponding algorithm name in database that shares the same architecture as AC-4: first making CSP arc-consistency and then solving the resulting CSP. Thanks to COVID-19, everything was online in 2020. I had the opportunity to attend the only academic conference I have ever been to so far, SIGMOD 2020, which was served online for free. In the conference, there was a nice tutorial session, where they brought the name Yannakakis's algorithm (YA) [Yannakakis, 1981].

The laser-focused TTJ development started around 08/2020 after I quitted my full-time job at Amazon. The design requirement I received was to not only turn TreeTracker into a join algorithm but also into an iterator form. The biggest claim in Miranker's head, besides the optimality guarantee like YA, is "downwardly compatible". That is, unlike YA that requires a query plan to introduce a full reducer, TTJ is just like any other binary join operator - there is no extra surgery towards the query plan; TTJ can be treated as a physical join operator implementation and directly be used as a drop-in replacement of any join operator. The end result of the iterator-form TTJ was sent to PODS 2022 in 06/2021.

Note

All the submissions and corresponding reviews linked in text are organized into a table below.

The paper is not well-received. People think in general the paper is off-topic for PODS and we need to provide experimental evidence to show that the potential overhead exists in YA no longer exists in TTJ even though both YA and TTJ have the same bound 2. From theory perspective, we are optimizing some constants that are hidden in the usual RAM complexity model. One reviewer also accurately points out a big limitation of TTJ compared to YA is that it has to work on a left-deep plan 3 to have the optimality guarantee.

Note

To fully appreciate the difference between YA and TTJ, I would recommend to read Example 1 in Section 1 of SIGMOD 2025 submission. If you think iterator form of TTJ hinders your understanding of the algorithm, I would recommend to read Example 1.1 in Section 1 of CIDR 2025 submission, which presents TTJ algorithm in push-based model (See 1, 2).

After receving the rejection in 08/2021, there were a lot of debate on what the next steps should be. Miranker suggested I should translate TTJ into Datalog 4 before he was away from the research to start selling his house in Austin and building a new house in Utah. Without a clear roadmap for the next steps, I decided to gather a second opinion. I first spoke with Kexin Rong who I knew when she visited UT-Austin for a job talk in 02/2021. She suggested to address reviewers' comments directly by providing empirical evidence of TTJ. The second person I spoke to is Bailu Ding, who I interned with at Microsoft in the summer 2022.
